Temperature
✅ Yes! Too hot or too cold slows the reaction. Sweet spot is ~25–35°C for most plants.

Lesson Plan: Photosynthesis
Student-Centered Demo Lesson · Life Science · Grade 6–8 · 15 Minutes
Subject: Life Science / Biology
Grade: 6–8 (Ages 11–13)
Duration: 15 minutes
Approach: Inquiry-Based, Student-Centered
Teacher talk time: < 90 seconds total
Student activity time: ~13.5 minutes (90%+)
Learning Objectives
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
1. Identify the three ingredients and two products of photosynthesis through a sorting activity.
2. Construct the photosynthesis equation by placing components in the correct order.
3. Predict and explain how changing one variable (light, CO2, water, temperature) affects the rate of photosynthesis.
4. Connect photosynthesis to real-world issues (food chains, climate change).

Curriculum alignment: NGSS MS-LS1-6 · Construct a scientific explanation based on evidence for the role of photosynthesis in the cycling of matter and flow of energy into and out of organisms.
Lesson Structure — 5 Phases
PhaseTimeStudent ActivityTeacher RolePedagogy
1. Wonder 0–3 min Flip "ingredient cards" to test guesses. Partner discussion: "What happens if you remove light?" Pose one big question. Circulate. Listen. Do NOT explain yet. Activate prior knowledge, curiosity hook
2. Discover 3–7 min Sort ingredients vs. products (drag & drop). Build the equation by selecting pieces in order. One prompt only: "Sort, then build." Walk around, ask "Why did you place that there?" Inquiry, hands-on construction, peer learning
3. Experiment 7–11 min Use virtual simulator — change ONE factor at a time, record findings, identify the limiting factor. Ask probing questions: "What happens at 0 light? What did you predict?" Scientific method, variable control, data literacy
4. Apply 11–13 min 4-question quiz — answer alone, then compare with partner. Say: "No peeking — try alone first." After, facilitate brief pair discussion. Formative assessment, peer explanation
5. Reflect 13–15 min Exit ticket: explain photosynthesis in own words or connect to climate change. Cold-call 2–3 students to share. Affirm and bridge to next lesson. Metacognition, real-world connection, transfer
Key Vocabulary
Core Terms
Photosynthesis Chlorophyll Chloroplast Glucose Stomata Autotroph Limiting factor
The Equation (students build this)

Differentiation Strategies
Support (Lower readiness)
- Wonder cards provide immediate feedback after flip
- Sort activity uses color coding (green = in, blue = out)
- Equation builder has wrong options clearly labeled
- Sentence starters provided for reflection
- Allow pair work throughout
Extension (Higher readiness)
- Explain why CO2 + water make glucose at the molecular level
- Research: how does shade adaptation work in plants?
- Connect to: why does ocean algae produce 50% of Earth's O2?
- Challenge: design an experiment to test a plant's light tolerance
Assessment
Formative (During lesson)
- Sort activity: observe who places products vs ingredients correctly
- Equation builder: check completion — who needed to reset?
- Simulator: listen for "limiting factor" language in student discussion
- Quiz: 4 questions with instant feedback — aim for 3+/4 class average
- Exit ticket: collect written reflections for review
Success Criteria (Students can...)
✓ Name 3 ingredients and 2 products of photosynthesis
✓ Write or recall the chemical equation
✓ Explain what a limiting factor is with one example
✓ Connect photosynthesis to at least one real-world issue
Student-Centered Design Principles Applied
1. Minimal teacher talk: Total teacher speaking time < 90 seconds. Each phase opens with one sentence of instruction maximum.
2. Learning by doing: Every concept is discovered through an activity — flip cards, drag & drop, build equations, run experiments, answer quizzes — before it is ever stated.
3. Immediate feedback: All activities provide instant, explanatory feedback so students self-correct without waiting for the teacher.
4. Social learning: Partner discussion built into Phases 1, 4, and 5. Peer comparison after quiz encourages explanation and solidifies understanding.
5. Real-world transfer: Every activity connects to a genuine application — food chains, greenhouse farming, climate change — to answer "why does this matter?"
Materials & Setup
- Device per student (or 1 per pair) with this webpage open
- Optional: whiteboard for students to draw their findings
- Notebooks / paper for written reflections
- Timer running on main screen (built into this page)
- No printed materials required — fully digital

Room setup: Pairs preferred. Groups of 3 acceptable. No rows — discussion is key.
